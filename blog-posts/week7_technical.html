It's worth understanding that optimization is about reductions in a variety of things or increases in others-- here, it's worth looking from a novice perspective at what it might mean to optimize queries.

First, let's recognize the most valuable resource is likely a coder's time, because it's so limited. Sure, milliseconds will scare cat luvin' video patrons away like ebola frenzied fear mongering 'mericuns, but in the real world, you and I are not going to be optimizing for milliseconds at first-- we want our code to run first.

Still, optimization of the length of time to return queries is an aggregate value, based on memory allocation, choice of query syntax, interconnections, CPU path length, disk storage, and other gobbledygook; but, the differences in possible ways to query can result in order of magnitudes of differences in time.

If optimization is so far out of the scope of reasonable topics, why consider it here. Because, like so many other things, it's a moving target and my growth as an engineer is progressing steadily. Suffice to say, it's enough to be aware that optimization of queries is a complicated topic and if I'm setting up or working a small or large db, and finding horrendous results, perhaps query time is the culprit and query optimization is the answer. Or, perhaps, someone left a semi-colon out somewhere?
